

1) Lanark Blue Cheese: A Farmhouse Classic from Scotland
Discover the iconic Lanark Blue cheese, a modern classic created by Selina Errington in Carnwath, Scotland. This rich and powerful cheese is Scotland's answer to Roquefort, with a history dating back to the 1980s.

2) Creamy and Flavorful: Lanark Blue's Characteristics
This creamy cheese is made with rich sheep's milk and boasts a strong blue veining that gives it a powerful kick. Its shape and size are reminiscent of traditional blue cheeses, with a weight that makes it perfect for sharing. Lanark Blue is made with the finest Lacaune dairy sheep and rennet, ensuring the highest quality ingredients.

3) A Time-Honored Process: How Lanark Blue is Made
Lanark Blue is made using a recipe from the 1820s written by Sir Walter Scott. The sheep's milk is sourced from a 450-strong flock on the rugged Scottish mountainside, giving the cheese its unique flavor. The cheese is unpressed and aged to perfection, following a process similar to that of Roquefort.

4) Bold and Distinctive: The Smell and Taste of Lanark Blue
Lanark Blue has a powerful aroma and a distinctive flavor that comes from the rich sheep's milk and aging process. This cheese is not for the faint of heart, but for those who appreciate a bold and flavorful cheese.

5) Perfectly Paired: Serving Suggestions for Lanark Blue
For the best experience, serve Lanark Blue at room temperature and present it on a cheese board with crackers, fruits, and nuts. This cheese pairs well with full-bodied red wines, adding a savory element to any meal. It also makes a delicious addition to salads and sandwiches.

6) Award-Winning Quality: Trust in Lanark Blue's Reputation
Don't just take our word for it - Lanark Blue has received numerous awards and accolades, solidifying its reputation as a top-quality cheese. As Humphrey Errington, the pioneer of Scottish cheese-making, once said, "The proof of the cheese is in the eating." Try Lanark Blue for yourself and taste the quality and craftsmanship that goes into every bite.
